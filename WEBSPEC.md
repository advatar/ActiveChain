SECTION 4 — THE SEVEN NATIVE PRIMITIVES

Headline:
A protocol that understands authority.

Intro:
Every state transition is expressed through seven native concepts. These concepts are available to wallets, smart contracts, organizations, autonomous agents, compute workers, and privacy proofs.

Create an interactive seven-card grid.

1. PRINCIPAL
A persistent identity for a person, organization, device, service, pseudonym, or AI agent.

A principal is controlled by a policy rather than permanently tied to one key. It can rotate cryptography, recover from compromise, and operate through private, pairwise identities.

2. CREDENTIAL
A signed claim made by an issuer about a principal.

Credentials can describe roles, qualifications, memberships, device properties, model evaluations, data rights, or institutional authority. Holders can prove selected facts without revealing the full credential.

3. CAPABILITY
A scoped grant of authority.

Capabilities can limit actions, objects, data, money, compute, tools, time, rate, use count, and delegation depth. A delegated capability may become narrower, but never more powerful.

4. POLICY
A deterministic rule defining when an action is permitted.

Policies combine credentials, capabilities, approvals, budgets, time constraints, risk conditions, and disclosure requirements. Policies can be public or satisfied privately in zero knowledge.

5. OBJECT
A versioned unit of state, ownership, or value.

Assets, contracts, identities, capabilities, datasets, organizations, private records, and model manifests are represented as explicit objects with visible dependency and authorization boundaries.

6. JOB
An asynchronous request for expensive computation.

AI inference, scientific computation, data processing, rendering, training, and external tool activity become authorized jobs with declared budgets, inputs, privacy requirements, and verification policies.

7. PROOF OR RECEIPT
Machine-verifiable evidence explaining why a result should be accepted.

A result may carry an exact validity proof, replicated-execution certificate, challengeable receipt, hardware attestation, or named evaluator approval. The assurance type is always explicit.

SECTION 16 — FAQ

Create an accessible accordion with these questions and answers.

QUESTION:
Is ActiveChain a blockchain?

ANSWER:
It is a global, consensus-backed object ledger, but its architecture differs from a conventional blockchain. Consensus orders available actions, while specialized executors and provers produce proof-carrying state transitions. Ordinary nodes verify the result without having to repeat every computation.

QUESTION:
Why not build this on an existing chain?

ANSWER:
Existing chains provide valuable consensus and settlement foundations, but their deepest abstractions were usually fixed before programmable identity, private authorization, post-quantum security, proof-carrying execution, and autonomous agents became central requirements.

Adding each property through a separate layer produces fragmented semantics and trust assumptions. A clean-slate design allows the state model, authorization system, virtual machine, privacy system, economics, and proof statement to be designed together.

QUESTION:
Does it support smart contracts?

ANSWER:
Yes. Smart contracts are native and operate over versioned, resource-safe objects. Contracts receive explicit object inputs and a verified authorization context. Bounded interactions remain atomically composable, while expensive AI and compute tasks use asynchronous jobs.

QUESTION:
Is it fully post-quantum?

ANSWER:
The goal is that no known quantum-vulnerable primitive is required in the core path for state authorization, consensus finality, protected transaction handling, or execution verification. The design uses standardized post-quantum signatures and key establishment together with transparent hash-based proofs. Cryptographic agility remains essential because no algorithm can be guaranteed secure forever.

QUESTION:
How does privacy work?

ANSWER:
Privacy is supported at several layers: protected transaction contents, private credentials, pairwise identities, private authorization policies, shielded objects, selective disclosure, and private compute inputs. Zero-knowledge proofs allow users to demonstrate that rules were satisfied without disclosing all underlying information.

QUESTION:
Does the chain run AI models directly?

ANSWER:
Small deterministic computations may be proved directly, but large models should not block consensus. AI work is submitted as an authorized compute job. External workers perform the computation and return the evidence required by the job’s verification policy.

QUESTION:
Does a proof show that an AI answer is true?

ANSWER:
No. A validity proof can show that a particular committed model and runtime produced a particular output from committed inputs. It does not establish that the output is factually correct, fair, safe, or socially beneficial.

QUESTION:
Who is allowed to participate?

ANSWER:
The design is permissionless at the infrastructure level. Validators, builders, provers, storage providers, relayers, credential issuers, evaluators, and AI workers can participate subject to the objective rules and bonds of their roles. Applications choose which credentials and evidence providers they trust.

QUESTION:
Can governance change balances or override contracts?

ANSWER:
The design includes no omnipotent state-edit key. Protocol upgrades require explicit software and protocol adoption. An emergency mechanism may halt the admission of new work, but it cannot fabricate a proof, seize assets, or silently replace application code.

QUESTION:
What remains unsolved?

ANSWER:
The architecture cannot eliminate stake concentration, network partitions, dishonest real-world issuers, compromised user devices, misleading oracles, all forms of MEV, AI alignment failures, or governance capture. It is designed to isolate these risks, make assumptions visible, and prevent specialized infrastructure from silently acquiring additional authority.

COPY STYLE

The voice should be:
- visionary but grounded;
- technically credible;
- calm rather than promotional;
- ambitious without claiming inevitability;
- understandable to an intelligent non-specialist;
- precise enough to retain the interest of technical readers.

Prefer:
“designed to”
“the goal is”
“the architecture separates”
“the protocol makes explicit”
“the system can prove”

Avoid:
“guaranteed”
“unhackable”
“infinitely scalable”
“solves trust”
“eliminates all MEV”
“fully decentralized” without measurement
“the fastest”
“revolutionary”
“world-changing” as an unsupported adjective
“Web3” as the primary category

Use short, declarative statements for major headlines and more detailed explanatory prose below them.

FINAL CREATIVE DIRECTION

The page should leave the visitor with three conclusions:

1. Existing internet and blockchain foundations were not designed for autonomous agents, private programmable identity, post-quantum security, and verifiable computation.

2. Starting from first principles allows identity, authorization, privacy, smart contracts, AI, economics, and formal verification to share one coherent security model.

3. ActiveChain is not asking users to trust a new class of powerful intermediaries. It is designing a system in which authority is explicit, computation carries evidence, and specialized providers remain replaceable.
